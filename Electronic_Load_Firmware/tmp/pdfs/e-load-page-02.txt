A) The control loop (makes the current constant)
Your op-amp compares the shunt voltage to I_SET and drives the MOSFET gates (that’s the heart of the load). 
eload-schematics
For that loop, you often don’t need a dedicated “current sense amplifier” because the op-amp is already acting 
on the shunt voltage.
B) The measurement loop (reports current/voltage/power accurately)
If your shunt drop is only 10–50 mV, the MCU ADC may struggle to measure it accurately without 
gain/CMRR.
So you have three good options:
Amplify shunt with an op-amp into the ADC (gain 20–100×), careful with offset + drift.
Use a dedicated current shunt monitor like INA226 (digital readout of shunt V + bus V over I²C). The INA226 
explicitly monitors shunt drop and bus voltage and can compute current and power.

+1
Use an external higher-res ADC (you already have ADS1115 in the reference design, but you also mentioned 
switching plans).
Important: INA226 does not replace your DAC
INA226 is measurement, not a setpoint generator. Your DAC (MCP4725) is what creates I_SET in your 
schematic. 
eload-schematics
So: INA226 does not replace DAC; it replaces (or improves) your current/voltage measurement chain
8) “RESET” and “FAULT/STOP”: where do they go (and what MCU pins)?
Treat them as two nets:
FAULT (latched hardware fault)
Comes from comparator → SR latch output.
Goes to:
Hard disable: kills gate drive (GATE_DISABLE into the BJT/op-amp stage 
enable)
MCU input: any GPIO with EXTI interrupt (so firmware can immediately log & 
stop profiles)
RESET
Drives SR latch reset input.
Also optionally drives MCU reset pin (or just the MCU can reset the latch via a 
GPIO).
Rule: Hardware fault shutdown must work even if the MCU is dead. The MCU 
only observes and records.
